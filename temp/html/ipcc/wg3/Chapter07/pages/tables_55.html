2 Besides emissions, and possible avoided emissions, related to the supply chain, the GHG effects of using bioenergy depend on:\n(i) change in GHG emissions when bioenergy substitutes another energy source; and (ii) how the associated land use and possible land-\nuse change influence the amount of carbon that is stored in vegetation and (Calvin et al. 2021) soils over time. Studies arrive at varying\nmitigation potentials for bioenergy and BECCS due to the large diversity of bioenergy systems, and varying conditions concerning\nwhere and how they are deployed (Elshout 2015; Harper et al.2018; Muri 2018; Kalt et al.2019; Brandão et al. 2019; Buchspies et al.\n2020; Cowie et al. 2021; Calvin et al. 2021). Important factors include feedstock type, land management practice, energy conversion\nefficiency, type of bioenergy product (and possible co-products), emissions intensity of the products being displaced, and the land use/\ncover prior to bioenergy deployment (Zhu et al. 2017; Staples et al. 2017; Daioglou et al. 2017; Carvalho et al. 2017; Hanssen et al.\n2020; Mouratiadou et al. 2020). Studies arrive at contrasting conclusions also when similar bioenergy systems and conditions are\nanalysed, due to different methodologies, assumptions, and parametrization (Harper et al.2018; Kalt et al.2019; Brandão et al. 2019;\nAlbers et al. 2019; Buchspies et al. 2020; Bessou et al. 2020; Rolls and Forster 2020; Cowie et al. 2021).
