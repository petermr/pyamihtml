0 Box 7.1 | Case Study: Reducing the Impacts of Roads on Deforestation
1
2 Summary
3 Rapidly expanding roads, particularly in tropical regions, are linked to forest loss, degradation, and fragmentation because the land
4 becomes more generally accessible. Increase of land values of areas adjacent to roads also drives speculation and deforestation\nrelated to land tenure (Fearnside 2015). If poorly planned, infrastructure can facilitate fires, illegal mining, and wildlife poaching with\nconsequences for GHG emissions and biodiversity conservation. However, some initiatives are providing new approaches for better\nplanning and then limit environmental and societal impacts.
5
6 Background
7 Although the number and extent of protected areas has increased markedly in recent decades (Watson et al. 2014), many other
8 indicators reveal that nature is in broad retreat. For example, the total area of intact wilderness is declining rapidly worldwide (Watson\net al. 2016), 70% of the world’s forests are now less than 1 km from a forest edge (Haddad et al. 2015), the extent of tropical forest\nfragmentation is accelerating exponentially (Taubert et al. 2018). One of the most direct and immediate driver of deforestation and\nbiodiversity decline is the dramatic expansion of roads and other transportation infrastructure (Laurance et al. 2014a; Laurance\nand Arrea 2017; Alamgir et al. 2017).
9
10 Case description
11 From 2010 to 2050, the total length of paved roads is projected to increase by 25 million km (Dulac 2013) including large infrastructure-
12 expansion schemes in Asia (Laurance and Arrea 2017; Lechner et al. 2018) and in South America (Laurance et al. 2001; Killeen 2007),\nas well as widespread illegal or unplanned road building (Laurance et al. 2009; Barber et al. 2014). For example, in the Amazon,\n95% of all deforestation occurs within 5.5 km of a road, and for every km of legal road there are nearly three km of illegal roads\n(Barber et al. 2014).
13
14 Interactions and limitations
15 More than any other proximate factor, the dramatic expansion of roads is determining the pace and patterns of habitat disruption and
16 its impacts on biodiversity (Laurance et al. 2009; Laurance and Arrea 2017). Much road expansion is poorly planned. Environmental\nImpact Assessments (EIAs) for roads and other infrastructure are typically too short term and superficial to detect rare species or\nassess long-term or indirect impacts of projects (Flyvbjerg 2009; Laurance and Arrea 2017). Another limitation is the consideration\nof each project in isolation from other existing or planned developments (Laurance et al. 2014b). Hence, EIAs alone are inadequate\nfor planning infrastructure projects and assessing their broader environmental, social, and financial impacts and risks (Laurance et al.\n2015a; Alamgir et al. 2017, 2018).
17
18 Lessons
19 The large-scale, proactive land-use planning is an option for managing the development of modern infrastructure. Approaches such
20 as the ‘Global Roadmap’ scheme (Laurance and Balmford 2013; Laurance et al. 2014a) Strategic Environmental Assessments (Fischer\n2007) can be used to evaluate the relative costs and benefits of infrastructure projects, and to spatially prioritise land uses to optimise\nhuman benefits while limited new infrastructure in areas of intact or critical habitats. For example, the Global Roadmap strategy has\nbeen used in parts of South-East Asia (Sloan et al. 2018), Indochina (Balmford et al. 2016), and sub-Saharan Africa (Laurance et al.\n2015b) to devise land-use zoning that can help optimise the many risks and rewards of planned infrastructure projects.
