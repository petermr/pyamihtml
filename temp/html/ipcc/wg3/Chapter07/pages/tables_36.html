0 Box 7.2 | Climate-smart Forestry in Europe
1
2 Summary
3 European forests have been regarded as prospering and increasing for the last five decades. However, these views also changed
4 recently. Climate change is putting a large pressure on mono species and high stocked areas of Norway spruce in Central Europe\n(Hlásny et al. 2021; Senf and Seidl 2021) with estimates of mortality reaching 200 million m3, biodiversity under pressure, the\nMediterranean area showing a weak sector and harvesting pressure in the Baltics and north reaching maxima achievable. A European\nstrategy for unlocking the EU’s forests and forest sector potential was needed at the time of developing the LULUCF regulation and\nwas based on the concept of ‘climate-smart forestry’ (CSF) (Nabuurs et al. 2017; Verkerk et al. 2020).
5
6 Background
7 The idea behind CSF is that it considers the whole value chain from forest to wood products and energy, illustrating that a wide range
8 of measures can be applied to provide positive incentives for more firmly integrating climate objectives into the forest and forest\nsector framework. CSF is more than just storing carbon in forest ecosystems; it builds upon three main objectives; (i) reducing and/\nor removing GHG emissions; (ii) adapting and building diverse forests for forest resilience to climate change; and (iii) sustainably\nincreasing forest productivity and incomes. These three CSF objectives can be achieved by tailoring policy measures and actions to\nregional circumstances in member states’ forest sectors.
