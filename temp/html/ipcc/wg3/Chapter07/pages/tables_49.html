0 Box 7.4 | Case Study: The Climate-smart Village Approach
1
2 Summary
3 The climate-smart villages (CSV) approach aims to generate local knowledge, with the involvement of farmers, researchers, practitioners,
4 and governments, on climate change adaptation and mitigation while improving productivity, food security, and farmers’ livelihoods\n(Aggarwal et al. 2018). This knowledge feeds a global network that includes 36 climate-smart villages in South and South-East Asia,\nWest and East Africa, and Latin America.
5
6 Background
7 It is expected that agricultural production systems across the world will change in response to climate change, posing significant
8 challenges to the livelihoods and food security of millions of people (Kennedy et al. 2014). Maintaining agricultural growth while\nminimising climate shocks is crucial to building a resilient food production system and meeting sustainable development goals in\nvulnerable countries.
9
10 Case description
11 The CSV approach seeks an integrated vision so that sustainable rural development is the final goal for rural communities. At the
12 same time, it fosters the understanding of climate change with the implementation of adaptation and mitigation actions, as much as\npossible. Rural communities and local stakeholders are the leaders of this process, where scientists facilitate their knowledge to be\nuseful for the communities and learn at the same time about challenges but also the capacity those communities have built through\ntime. The portfolio includes weather-smart activities, water-smart practices, seed/breed smart, carbon-/nutrient-smart practices, and\ninstitutional-/market-smart activities.
13
14 Interactions and limitations
15 The integration of technologies and services that are suitable for the local conditions resulted in many gains for food security and
16 adaptation and for mitigation where appropriate. It was also shown that, in all regions, there is considerable yield advantage when\na portfolio of technologies is used, rather than the isolated use of technologies (Govaerts et al. 2005; Zougmoré et al. 2014). Moreover,\nfarmers are using research results to promote their products as climate-smart leading to increases in their income (Acosta-Alba\net al. 2019). However, climatic risk sites and socio-economic conditions together with a lack of resource availability are key issues\nconstraining agriculture across all five regions.
17
18 Lessons
19 i. Understanding the priorities, context, challenges, capacity, and characteristics of the territory and the communities regarding\nclimate, as well as the environmental and socio-economic dimensions, is the first step. Then, understanding climate vulnerability\nin their agricultural systems based on scientific data but also listening to their experience will set the pathway to identify climate-\nsmart agriculture (CSA) options (practices and technologies) to reduce such vulnerability.
20 ii. Building capacity is also a critical element of the CSV approach, rural families learn about the practices and technologies in
21 a neighbour’s house, and as part of the process, families commit to sharing their knowledge with other families, to start a scaling-\nout process within the communities. Understanding the relationship between climate and their crop is key, as well as the use of\nweather forecasts to plan their agricultural activities.
22 The assessment of the implementation of the CSA options should be done together with community leaders to understand changes\nin livelihoods and climate vulnerability. Also, knowledge appropriation by community leaders has led to farmer-to-farmer knowledge\nexchange within and outside the community (Ortega Fernandez and Martínez-Barón 2018).
