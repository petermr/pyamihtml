0 Box 7.3 | Case Study: Agroforestry in Brazil – CANOPIES
1
2 Summary
3 Brazilian farmers are integrating trees into their croplands in various ways, ranging from simple to highly complex agroforestry
4 systems. While complex systems are more effective in the mitigation of climate change, trade-offs with scalability need to be resolved\nfor agroforestry systems to deliver on their potential. The Brazilian-Dutch CANOPIES project (Janssen 2020) is exploring transition\npathways to agroforestry systems optimised for local ecological and socio-economic conditions.
5
6 Background
7 The climate change mitigation potential of agroforestry systems is widely recognised (Zomer et al. 2016; FAO 2017b) and Brazilian
8 farmers and researchers are pioneering diverse ways of integrating trees into croplands, from planting rows of eucalyptus trees in\npastures up to highly complex agroforests consisting of >30 crop and tree species. The degree of complexity influences the multiple\nfunctions that farmers and societies can attain from agroforestry: the more complex it is, the more it resembles a natural forest with\nassociated benefits for its carbon storage capacity and its habitat quality for biodiversity (Santos et al. 2019). However, trade-offs\nexist between the complexity and scalability of agroforestry as complex systems rely on intensive manual labour to achieve high\nproductivity (Tscharntke et al. 2011). To date, mechanisation of structurally diverse agroforests is scarce and hence, efficiencies of\nscale are difficult to achieve.
9
10 Case description
11 These synergies and trade-offs between complexity, multifunctionality and scalability are studied in the CANOPIES (Co-existence of
12 Agriculture and Nature: Optimisation and Planning of Integrated Ecosystem Services) project, a collaboration between Wageningen\nUniversity (NL), the University of São Paulo and EMBRAPA (both Brazil). Soil and management data are collected on farms of varying\ncomplexity to evaluate carbon sequestration and other ecosystem services, economic performance and labour demands.
13
14 Interactions and limitations
15 The trade-off between complexity and labour demand is less pronounced in EMBRAPA’s integrated crop-livestock-forestry (ICLF)
16 systems, where grains and pasture are planted between widely spaced tree rows. Here, barriers for implementation relate mostly to\nlivestock and grain farmers’ lack of knowledge on forestry management and financing mechanisms5 (Gil et al. 2015). Additionally, linking\nthese financing mechanisms to carbon sequestration remains a Monitoring, Reporting and Verification challenge (Smith et al. 2020b).
