0 Box 8.3: Coordination of Fragmented Policymaking for Low-carbon Urban Development:\nExample from Shanghai, China
1
2 As a growing megacity in the Global South, Shanghai represents the challenge of becoming low carbon despite its economic growth\nand population size (Chen et al. 2017). Shanghai was designated as one of the pilot low-carbon cities by the central government.\nThe city utilised a coordination mechanism for joining fragmented policymaking across the city’s economy, energy, and environment.\nThe coordination mechanism was supported by a direct fund that enabled implementation of cross-sector policies beyond a single-\nsector focus across multiple institutions while increasing capacity for enabling a low-carbon transition for urban sustainability\n(Peng and Bai 2020).
3
4 Implementation and governance process
5 In Shanghai, coordination between the central and local governments had an instrumental role for encouraging low-carbon policy
6 experimentation. Using a nested governance framework, the central government provided target setting and performance evaluation\nwhile the local government initiated pilot projects for low-carbon development. The policy practices in Shanghai surpassed the top-\ndown targets and annual reporting of GHG emissions, including carbon labelling standards at the local level, pilot programme for\ntransitioning sub-urban areas, and the engagement of public utilities (Peng and Bai 2018).
7
8 Towards low-carbon urban development
9 New policy measures in Shanghai were built upon a series of related policies from earlier, ranging from general energy saving
10 measures to air pollution reduction. This provided a continuum of policy learning for implementing low-carbon policy measures.\nAn earlier policy was a green electricity scheme based on the Jade Electricity Program while the need for greater public awareness was\none aspect requiring further attention in policy design (Baeumler et al. 2012), supporting policy-learning for policies later on. The key\npoint here is that low-carbon policies were built on and learned from earlier policies with similar goals.
11
12 Outcomes and impacts of the policy mix
13 Trends during 1998 and 2015 indicate that energy intensity decreased from about 130 tonnes per million RMB to about 45 tonnes
14 per million RMB and carbon intensity decreased from about 0.35 Mt per billion RMB to 0.10 Mt per billion RMB (Peng and Bai 2018).\nThese impacts on energy and carbon intensities represent progress, while challenges remain. Among the challenges are the need\nfor investment in low-carbon technology and increases in urban carbon sinks (Yang and Li 2018) while cross-sector interaction and\ncomplexity are increasing.
