Finding F5 shows that, metaphorically speaking, there is “exploitation” of the rich
countries by the poor, which is a seminal result in the theory of GPGs (Olson 1965, Olson and
Zeckhauser 1966, and Sandler 2015). Similarly, there is exploitation of countries that are more
interested in GPG provision by less interested countries (Buchholz and Sandler 2016). The
impacts of income and preferences may counteract each other so that, e.g., a country with low
income but strong preferences for the GPG contributes more than a country with high income but
low preferences.
When noncontributors are taken into account Warr neutrality ceases to apply, and income
transfers may yield an increase in public good supply (Bergstrom, Blume, and Varian 1986).
