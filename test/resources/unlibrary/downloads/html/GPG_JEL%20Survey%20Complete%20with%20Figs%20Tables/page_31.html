If some country i matches some other country j’s GPG contribution with the matching
rate is (and the GPG productivity of all countries is one), then country i augments any GPG
contribution of country j by ijsg. This changes matched country j’s marginal rate of
transformation between the private good and the GPG to 1is+, thereby making the private good
relatively more expensive. Henceforth, country j’s Nash equilibrium position is on the
expansion path (,1)jieGs+, which favors GPG provision compared to the original expansion
path. Matching country i remains on its original expansion path. Following the same reasoning
as applied above in other scenarios, the Nash equilibrium under matching must result in a larger
GPG supply, a smaller GPG contribution, a larger private consumption, and greater utility for the
matching country i. By contrast, the matched country j has a lower utility since its lost private
consumption is more detrimental than the resulting increase in GPG supply.29This outcome also
occurs if country i matches the GPG contributions of more than a single country.
